Lesson 3: Habitability and Life

3.1 Habitable Zone

  • Definition: The habitable zone is the region around a star where temperatures are just right for liquid water to exist on a planet's surface. This zone is also known as the "Goldilocks Zone."
  • Distance from Star: For our solar system, the habitable zone is approximately 0.95 to 1.37 AU (astronomical units) from the Sun, varying depending on the star's type, size, and age.
  • Factors Influencing Habitability:
    • Star Type: Different types of stars have different habitable zones; smaller, cooler stars have closer zones, while larger, hotter stars have more distant zones.
    • Star Size and Age: As stars age, their luminosity changes, shifting the habitable zone.

3.2 Biosignatures

  • Definition: Biosignatures are signs of life detected in the atmosphere or surface of a planet, including specific gases or molecules produced by living organisms.
  • Types of Biosignatures:
    • Oxygen: Often produced by photosynthetic organisms.
    • Methane: Can be produced by biological processes.
    • Water Vapor: Essential for life.
    • Biofluorescence: Some organisms emit light, detectable as a biosignature.
  • Detection Methods:
    • Spectroscopy: Analyzing light spectrum to identify specific gases.
    • Transit Spectroscopy: Observing light from a star as a planet passes in front of it, revealing the planet's atmospheric composition.

3.3 Conditions for Life

  • Liquid Water: Essential for all known forms of life, acting as a solvent for biochemical reactions.
  • Organic Molecules: Carbon-based molecules are the building blocks of life.
  • Energy Source: Life requires an energy source, like sunlight or chemical reactions.
  • Stable Environment: A consistent environment with stable conditions is crucial for sustaining life.
  • Factors Influencing Life:
    • Temperature: Must allow for liquid water.
    • Pressure: Must be sufficient to maintain liquid water.
    • Chemistry: Presence of essential elements like carbon, hydrogen, nitrogen, oxygen, phosphorus, and sulfur.

3.4 Extremophiles

  • Definition: Extremophiles thrive in extreme environments that would be hostile to most life forms.
  • Temperature Range: Extremophiles can survive in temperatures from -20°C to 120°C.
  • Pressure Range: Some can withstand pressures up to 1000 times that of Earth's surface.
  • Examples:
    • Thermophilic Bacteria: Thrive in hot environments like hydrothermal vents.
    • Tardigrades: Microscopic animals that can survive extreme temperatures, radiation, and vacuum of space.

3.5 Notable Exoplanets

Kepler-452b: An Earth-sized planet in the habitable zone of a G-type star, with a radius 1.6 times that of Earth and an orbital period of 385 days.
Proxima Centauri b: Closest known exoplanet to Earth, orbiting Proxima Centauri, located in the habitable zone, and has a mass at least 1.17 times that of Earth.
TRAPPIST-1 System: A star system with seven Earth-sized planets, three in the habitable zone, with potential for supporting liquid water.
KELT-9b: The hottest known exoplanet, with surface temperatures exceeding 4,000°C, orbiting very close to its star.
WASP-12b: The hottest transiting exoplanet, known for high temperatures and an elongated shape due to tidal forces.
